Featured exhibits

The exhibits are expanded versions of exhibits entered into competition at multiple philatelic shows. The first two completed are The Evolution of the Holocaust in Holland and False Hopes and Lasting Thanks: The Battle of Arnhem, with exhibits in development on Code Name Yellow: 1940-Invasion and Occupation of Holland, Daily Life in Occupied Holland, and The Hunger Winter and Liberation.

The Evolution of Holocaust in Holland

Germany invaded Holland on May 10, 1940 and the majority of Dutch forces surrendered 4 days later on May 14, 1940. During 1940, the German occupation authorities banned Jews from the civil service and required Jews to register the assets of their business enterprises. In January 1941, the German authorities required all Jews to register themselves. A total of 159,806 persons registered, including 19,561 persons born of mixed marriages. The total included some 25,000 Jewish refugees from the German Reich. A Jewish council was established in February 1941 and in the same month the first “razzia” or roundup of young Jewish men took place. From May 3, 1942 it became obligatory for Jewish citizens over the age of 6 to wear the Star of David. Jewish businesses were boycotted and vandalized.

This exhibit shows in detail how the destruction of the Jewish population in Holland evolved from pre-war Refugee Camps through a series of Transit and Concentration Camps to the gas chambers of Auschwitz and Sobibor. Using contemporaneous correspondence and related documents, the exhibit shows the clear progression from collection to internment and on to deportation to the “East”, ultimately resulting in the death of over 105,000 Dutch Jews. The vast majority of these Dutch civilians were interred at Transit Camp Westerbork, before being sent on weekly trains to their deaths. More than 100,000 Jewish persons spent time in Camp Westerbork. There were only 876 survivors left at Camp Westerbork when the camp was liberated in April 1945.

False Hopes and Lasting Thanks: The Battle of Arnhem

On September 17, the First Allied Airborne Army and XXX Corps of the Second British Army launched Operation Market Garden, the largest airborne operation of the war. Its objective was the creation of a bridgehead, stretching from the Belgian border and over the Rhine River, covering a distance of 64 mi (103 Km), thereby providing an invasion route into Northern Germany. U.S. and British airborne forces were to simultaneously capture and hold 9 bridges across 6 major waterways while the British Second Army would swiftly follow overland and secure the invasion route.

The Battle of Arnhem was fought in and around the Dutch city of Arnhem and the neighboring villages of Oosterbeek, Wolfheze, Renkum and Driel. The first elements of the 1st Airborne Division landed at midday on 17 September 1944, some 8 miles (13 km) from its objectives, the road and rail bridges. At first the troops made excellent progress, being welcomed as liberators by the jubilant Dutch civilians. However, its advance towards the road and railway bridges was hampered by unexpected resistance, especially from elements of the 9th and 10th SS Panzer Divisions.

Only a small force of approximately 750 men was able to reach the Arnhem road bridge. The advance of the main body of the division was stopped on the outskirts of the town. XXX Corps was unable to advance north from Nijmegen as quickly as planned and after four days, the small British force at the bridge was overwhelmed and what was left of the division became trapped in a small pocket in Oosterbeek, just north of the river.

After nine days of fighting, the remnants of the division were withdrawn across the Rhine in Operation Berlin. The 1st Airborne Division lost nearly three quarters of its strength and did not see combat again. The Allies were unable to advance further with no secure bridges over the Rhine and the front line stabilized south of Arnhem, extending the war in the North of Holland for another 8 months. This split between north and south severed postal and other connections. Despite its defeat, the battle of the British 1st Airborne Division at Arnhem has become the stuff of legends and the subject of numerous books, as well as the popular film “A Bridge Too Far.”

Code Name Yellow: 1940-Invasion and Occupation of Holland

Dutch Foreign Policy had always favored neutrality, doing what it could to avoid conflicts outside its borders. This approach had been successful during the First World War and the government went to great lengths not to antagonize the Nazi regime. However, this time this neutral approach did not work.

Following the German invasion of Poland in September 1939 and Denmark and Norway in April 1940, the Dutch hastily undertook a general mobilization. All leave was canceled and the Dutch government declared Martial Law on April 9th, 1940. However, German troops crossed the Dutch border in the pre-dawn hours of Friday, May 10th, 1940 as part of a larger campaign (Code Yellow) to secure the Low Lands, including Belgium and Luxembourg, thereby denying the British a foothold on the European mainland.

Dutch forces were no match for the Germans and they were forced to surrender after just five days. Queen Wilhelmina fled to England on a British warship and though she was strongly criticized at the time, she soon became a potent symbol of the fight against Germany. (Available Spring 2024).

Life in Occupied Holland

The German occupation of the Netherlands spanned almost exactly five years, from May 1940 through May 1945. This exhibit and collection will focus on the lives of the Dutch people under occupation and the impact on their daily lives of the increasingly stringent regulations enforced by the Nazis. Topics explored will include censorship, rationing, resistance and forced labor. (Available Winter 2024).

The Hunger Winter and Liberation

After the failure of Operation Market Garden, the Netherlands was divided between liberated areas in the south and those still occupied by the Germans in the north. As an unusually cold winter began, the Germans reacted to Dutch railway strikes in support of the Allies and civilians in occupied areas were cut off from food supplies and forced to survive on restricted rations, resulting in starvation and hypothermia. In liberated areas, people were busily trying to put their lives back together, while searching for family members and news of those still living under occupation. (Available Winter 2024).
